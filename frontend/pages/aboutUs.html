Overview

This project aims to predict Aerosol Optical Depth (AOD), a key indicator of air pollution and atmospheric particulate matter, using a range of environmental and meteorological parameters such as wind speed, temperature, cloud cover, soil moisture, and humidity. The approach involves collecting multi-source data from satellite products like MODIS for AOD and NDVI, and reanalysis datasets such as ERA5 or MERRA-2 for weather-related variables. After preprocessing the data to align spatial and temporal resolutions and handling missing values, machine learning models—such as Random Forests or XGBoost—are trained to learn the relationship between these input features and AOD levels. The model is validated using standard metrics like RMSE and R², and further analyzed to understand the influence of each variable. The final output provides a predictive tool capable of estimating AOD across space and time, which can support air quality monitoring and environmental policy decisions.

How it Works

The project uses a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to predict Aerosol Optical Depth (AOD) by learning spatial patterns from environmental data such as wind speed, temperature, cloud cover, soil moisture, and related variables. These input parameters are formatted as multi-channel images or grids that represent different environmental layers over a geographic region. The CNN processes these inputs through convolutional layers that extract spatial features, capturing local and regional interactions among variables. By learning from historical data with known AOD values, the model identifies complex, nonlinear relationships between the input features and AOD levels. Once trained, the CNN can predict AOD for new environmental conditions, offering high-resolution, spatially-aware forecasts that are useful for air quality assessment and environmental monitoring.

Input Parameters for AOD Prediction using CNN

Parameter Description
zust(Friction Velocity) A measure of atmospheric turbulence and wind shear near the ground.
BA (Biomass Burning/Burning Area) Indicates the presence and intensity of fire activity, a major source of aerosols.
Land Surface Temperature (LST) Thermal emission from the land surface
tsr (Top of Atmosphere Shortwave Radiation) The total amount of solar energy received at the top of the atmosphere.
tisr (Top of Atmosphere Incident Solar Radiation) The amount of incoming solar radiation before it interacts with the atmosphere.
t at 1000 hPa (Temperature at 1000 hPa) Air temperature at a pressure level very close to the Earth's surface.
d at 850 hPa (Dew Point at 850 hPa) A measure of moisture content in the lower-to-mid atmosphere (around 1.5 km altitude).
NDVI (Normalized Difference Vegetation Index) A satellite-derived metric that quantifies the health and density of green vegetation.
ptype (Precipitation Type) The form of precipitation, such as rain, snow, or sleet, which can wash aerosols out of the air.
ws_100m (Wind Speed at 100 meters) The speed of the wind at 100 meters above the ground, affecting aerosol transport
